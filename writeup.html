Catching the Spider

Notes from fitting the first public J-lens for Llama-3.1-8B, by a sales engineer who learned what a logprob was three weeks ago. July 2026.
TL;DR Anthropic's workspace paper (July 6, 2026) showed you can read a model's unspoken intermediate thoughts with a "Jacobian lens." I ported the lens to Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct - as far as I can tell, the first public fit for a Llama model - for about $14 of rented GPU. It works: ask the model how many legs "the animal that spins webs" has and the token spider becomes the #1-ranked readout (of 128,256) in layer 20, at the word "webs," while the model just answers "eight." There is an interactive demo. Part 2 - a preregistered test of whether workspace readouts predict answer correctness better than logprob confidence - is running as I write this, and I will publish the result whether it is positive, null, or negative.

How I got here

I work in customer-facing engineering at a company that sells compliance software to medical device manufacturers. Our customers' auditors ask a question that AI systems currently cannot answer: "show me the decision process, not just the decision." When Anthropic published Verbalizable Representations Form a Global Workspace in Language Models, I spent a week doing adversarial market research on products you could build on it. Every verdict came back "not now" - the science was days old and only partially replicated. But one cheap move stayed valuable in every branch: run the experiment myself, on hardware I rent, on a model whose insides I am allowed to look at. So I did, with an AI assistant writing the code and me operating the terminal (more on that below).

What the lens is, in one paragraph

A language model's "thinking" at any moment is a list of 4,096 numbers per layer - meaningful to the model, gibberish to us. The Jacobian lens is a translation dictionary, built by measuring (via calculus, on ordinary text) how nudging each middle layer's state would change the model's final word choice. Point the fitted lens at any internal state and it tells you which vocabulary words that state is pushing toward. Anthropic fitted it to Claude; the released code ships with Qwen examples; nobody had published a fit for Llama.

What I actually did

  • Verified the released code end-to-end on its default model (Qwen), where the two-hop "currency of the country shaped like a boot" prompt surfaced Italy at layer 16 - the advertised behavior, reproduced.
  • Fitted the lens for Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct on a rented A100: 100 wikitext prompts, 9.5 hours overnight, checkpointed. The convergence diagnostic fell from 0.62 to 0.02 across the run - the textbook "settling average" curve.
  • Built the piece the released repo does not include: batched gradient pursuit, the sparse decomposition that expresses an internal state as a nonnegative recipe of at most 25 dictionary atoms. Validated on planted synthetic recipes: 100% support recovery on a toy dictionary, 99.7% on the real 128,256-atom dictionary (which contains genuinely confusable near-twins like spider/spiders). It runs at 1.6 ms per decomposition, which makes the full Part 2 dataset about one GPU-hour.

The spider

The acceptance test, straight from the paper's style: "Fact: The number of legs on the animal that spins webs is". The model answers "eight." The word spider appears nowhere in the prompt and nowhere in the output. But in the lens readout, spider is the single top-ranked token - rank 1 of 128,256 - at layer 20, exactly at the position of the word "webs," fading before the output layers. An unspoken thought, caught mid-flight, on a model running on a rented GPU, through a lens fitted overnight. Click around it yourself - every claim in this paragraph is inspectable.

One honest detail I want on the record because it taught me something: my first attempt failed. With a bare dangling prompt (no "Fact:" prefix), the instruct-tuned model read the sentence as a fill-in-the-blank worksheet - and the lens dutifully showed quiz-formatting concepts instead of spiders. The instrument was working; the mind it was reading was confused. Prompt framing matters, and the lens shows you when it does.

Why this might matter

Today, the standard machine signal for "should I trust this AI answer" is the model's own token confidence (logprob), plus asking it how sure it is - and models are notorious for being confidently wrong. The workspace paper suggests something better might exist: deliberation you can observe directly. Whether observed deliberation actually predicts correctness on standard benchmarks is - to my knowledge - unmeasured. That is Part 2: three preregistered workspace scores (answer-concept routing, workspace stability, question-coverage) against logprob and verbalized-confidence baselines, on GSM8K and TriviaQA, scored by AUC with bootstrap error bars and a shuffled-label control. The scoring definitions and decision rules were frozen before any benchmark data was generated. The generation run is executing tonight; the result gets published here regardless of what it says.

What could be wrong with this

  • The lens fit is the community "cheap path" (100 prompts, approximate), not the paper's exact 1000-prompt Jacobian spec.
  • Lens atoms are single vocabulary tokens by construction; multi-token concepts are anchored by first/digit tokens. This is the method's published limitation, and it is why numeric GSM8K is the primary dataset.
  • 8B is small; the only independent replication (Nanda, on Qwen 27B) found weaker and noisier effects than the paper. A null at this scale is a live possibility, and the prereg treats it as a reportable result, not a failure.
  • One model family, one lens fit, one operator. Replicate before trusting.

What's next

The Part 2 AUC table, with whatever it says. If workspace scores beat logprob baselines by the preregistered margin on both datasets, that is early evidence for deliberation-based trust signals. If they do not, the first public measurement of that gap is still worth having. Either way the repo, the demo, and this page stay up.
